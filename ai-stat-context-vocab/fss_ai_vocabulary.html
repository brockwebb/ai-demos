How to read this

Each term has two layers. Layer 1 — the official definition tells you what the term means in the world, with its source shown. Layer 2 — the FSS context tells you what it means inside the federal statistical system. The two are always kept distinct, and every claim is labeled so you know what kind of claim it is.

Legend

★ Flagship — the project's headline term; its definition (grounded) and forward interpretation (ahead-of-policy) are shown separately.
⚠ False friend — means something different in AI than in FSS practice. Read these.
⚖ Contested — politically live policy context.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus — backed by cited corpus documents.
✎ Practitioner note — a best-read interpretation, NOT corpus authority.
⏩ Forward interpretation — dated, ahead of the documented standard; bounded from the definition.

Sourcing

Public, freely-accessible authority only. No paywalled sources (e.g. ISO/IEC standards behind a paywall are NOT used). Priority: US federal (NIST/OMB/statute) > open standards > open foreign-government or open industry/academic references > needs_source. A free, open definition always beats a gatekept one. source_quality is tagged honestly: where no US-federal definition exists, the record says so rather than dressing a foreign/industry source as federal.

AI Core

14 terms

artificial intelligence

AI Core
also: AI
An umbrella term for software that does tasks we used to think needed a human — predicting, classifying, recommending, generating text — by finding patterns in data rather than following hand-written rules. There is no single legal definition; the statutory one (15 U.S.C. § 9401) is the federal anchor.
Definition
A machine-based system that can, for a given set of human-defined objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments.
— NIST SP 800-218A (per 15 U.S.C. § 9401(3)) U.S. federal
Also defined: Any artificial system that performs tasks under varying and unpredictable circumstances without significant human oversight, or that can learn from experience and improve performance when exposed to data sets; ... designed to think or act like a human ... or that is designed to act rationally. — Sec. 238(g), John S. McCain NDAA for FY2019 (10 U.S.C. § 2358 note) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Covers any automated analytical system a statistical agency runs — imputation models, record linkage, automated coding, disclosure-avoidance engines. The label matters because OMB AI guidance (M-25-21) attaches obligations to 'AI' use.
Cited in: eo_13859, eo_14110, m_24_10

AI system

AI Core
also: artificial intelligence system
The whole working setup — the data pipeline, the model, and the output logic — not just the model file. When you evaluate AI risk, you evaluate the system, because harms come from how the pieces fit together, not the model alone.
Definition
An engineered or machine-based system that can, for a given set of objectives, generate outputs such as predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments. AI systems are designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy.
— NIST AI 100-1 (AI Risk Management Framework 1.0) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Distinguishes the full deployed tool (e.g., a record-linkage system: input data + model + match-decision logic + output) from the model component. OMB risk-management practices apply to the system in use.
Cited in: eo_14110, nist_ai_rmf_100_1, m_24_18

AI model

AI Core
also: model
The trained mathematical object inside an AI system — the thing that turns inputs into outputs. Think of it like a fitted statistical model, but usually far larger and learned from data rather than specified by a methodologist.
Definition
A component of an AI system that implements AI technology and uses computational, statistical, or machine-learning techniques to produce outputs from a given set of inputs.
— OMB M-24-10 / NTIA AI Accountability Policy Report glossary U.S. federal
Crosswalk → fitted statistical model / estimator (analog_of): Both map inputs to outputs from data. Disanalogy: a statistical estimator has an explicit, documented functional form and known sampling properties; an AI model's form is learned and often opaque, so its error properties are empirical, not derived.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
In the FSS, 'AI models' include imputation models, disclosure-avoidance noise mechanisms, and predictive coding tools used in survey processing.
Cited in: eo_14319, m_24_10, m_24_18

machine learning

AI Core
also: ML
A way of building models by letting an algorithm find patterns in data and improve with more examples, instead of a person writing the rules. Most of what people call 'AI' today is machine learning.
Definition
The development and use of computer systems that adapt and learn from data with the goal of improving accuracy.
— NIST SP 800-55v1 U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Increasingly used in federal surveys for donor selection in imputation, nonresponse adjustment, and automated industry/occupation coding.
Cited in: m_24_18, eo_14110, m_24_10

generative AI

AI Core
also: GenAI, generative artificial intelligence
AI that produces new content — text, images, code, synthetic data — that looks like its training data but is newly generated, not retrieved from a database. Large language models are the most familiar kind.
Definition
The class of AI models that emulate the structure and characteristics of input data in order to generate derived synthetic content such as images, videos, audio, text, and other digital content.
— EO 14110 §3(p) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Introduces confabulation risk when drafting statistical narratives or metadata, and is an emerging tool for synthetic-data generation for privacy-protective dissemination.
Cited in: eo_14319, m_24_10, m_24_18

large language model

AI Core
also: LLM
A generative-AI model trained on very large amounts of text that predicts likely next words, letting it answer questions, summarize, translate, and draft. It is a fluent pattern-matcher over language, not a database of verified facts — which is why it can state wrong things confidently (see confabulation).
Definition
a class of language models that use deep-learning algorithms and are trained on extremely large textual datasets that can be multiple terabytes in size. LLMs can be classed into two types: generative or discriminatory.
— AI Assurance (2022), via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) academic
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The tool most FSS staff will actually touch. Useful for drafting and summarizing; unsafe for producing statistics or facts without verification. NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) is the governing risk reference — pull its exact definition before formal use.
Cited in: eo_14319, eo_14110, americas_ai_action_plan_2025

foundation model

AI Core
also: frontier model
A very large model pre-trained on broad data that serves as a reusable base others adapt (fine-tune) for specific tasks. Most commercial LLMs are foundation models.
Definition
In generative AI, models trained on broad data using self-supervised learning that can be adapted such as through fine-tuning for a variety of down­stream tasks.
— NIST AI 100-2e2025 U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
When an agency adopts a vendor foundation model, it inherits that model's training data, biases, and limitations — a value-chain risk that can collide with CIPSEA or SPD No. 15 category definitions.
Cited in: m_24_10, eo_14110, m_24_18

training data

AI Core
The examples a model learns from. The model's behavior, blind spots, and biases are inherited from this data — if a group is underrepresented in training data, the model tends to perform worse for that group.
Definition
A dataset from which a model is learned.
— AI Fairness 360, via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) academic
Crosswalk → sampling frame / sample coverage (analog_of): Both determine who is represented in what the system 'knows.' Disanalogy: a frame's coverage is measurable against a known target population; training-data coverage often has no enumerable target, so its gaps are harder to quantify.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Directly analogous to a sample frame's coverage: gaps in training data produce systematic errors the same way frame undercoverage does. FSS training data may come from administrative records or prior survey waves, each with CIPSEA and quality implications.
Cited in: nist_ai_rmf_100_1, advancing_american_ai_act_2022, americas_ai_action_plan_2025

inference

AI Core ⚠ False friend
also: model inference
In AI, 'inference' means running a trained model to get an output for a new input — the production step, after training. It does NOT mean drawing conclusions about a population.
Definition
The stage of ML in which a model is applied to a task. For example, a classifier model produces the classification of a test sample.
— NISTIR 8269 (draft), via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) U.S. federal
⚠ False friend — different in AI vs. the FSS
Statistical inference quantifies uncertainty about a population parameter from a sample. ML inference is the forward pass of a fitted model on new inputs and carries no population-level uncertainty claim by itself.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
A hard FALSE FRIEND. To a statistician, 'inference' means reasoning from a sample to a population (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests). To an ML engineer, 'inference' is just 'the model made a prediction.' Same word, different concept — confirm which sense is meant in any AI procurement or methods discussion.
Cited in: fcsm_20_04, m_24_10, m_25_21

model drift

AI Core
also: drift, concept drift
When a deployed model's accuracy decays over time because the world it sees no longer matches the world it was trained on (populations shift, definitions change, behavior changes). It degrades silently unless you monitor for it.
Definition
[concept drift] an online supervised learning scenario when the relation between the input data and the target variable changes over time.
— Gama et al. (concept drift), via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) academic
Also defined: Systems that classify or predict a concept (e.g., credit ratings or computer intrusion monitors) over time can suffer performance loss when the concept they are tracking changes. — Raynor, via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) academic
Also defined: Use of a system outside the planned domain of application, and a common cause of performance gaps between laboratory settings and the real world. — NIST SP 1270, via NIST AI 100-3 (AIRC glossary) U.S. federal
Crosswalk → temporal nonsampling error / frame obsolescence (analog_of): Both are degradation of a fixed instrument as the target population moves. Disanalogy: frame obsolescence is auditable against updated frames; model drift needs explicit performance monitoring to detect.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Closely analogous to a sampling frame going stale or mode effects shifting across survey cycles — the instrument slowly stops measuring what it used to.
Cited in: m_24_18, nist_ai_rmf_100_1, nist_ai_rmf_playbook

synthetic data

AI Core
also: synthetic microdata
Artificially generated records that mimic the statistical properties of real data without being any real person's actual record. Used for privacy-protective sharing, testing, and model training.
Definition
A process in which seed data are used to create artificial data that have some of the statistical characteristics of the seed data.
— NIST SP 800-188 U.S. federal
Also defined: a process in which seed data is used to create artificial data that has some of the statistical characteristics as the seed data — NISTIR 8053 U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
An emerging disclosure-avoidance tool: synthetic microdata products let researchers work with statistically representative data while protecting Title 13 / CIPSEA confidentiality. Utility and disclosure risk must both be assessed.
Cited in: acdeb_final_report_2022, icsp_private_datasets_2023, nist_genai_profile_600_1

differential privacy

AI Core ⚖ Contested
also: DP, formal privacy
A mathematical guarantee that adding or removing any one person's data barely changes what is published, so an attacker cannot reliably tell whether you were in the dataset. The protection is tunable by a 'privacy-loss budget' (epsilon): more privacy means more noise and less accuracy.
Definition
A rigorous mathematical definition of disclosure that considers the risk that an individual's confidential data may be learned as a result of a mathematical analysis based on that data being made publicly available.
— NIST SP 800-188 U.S. federal · contested
Also defined: A mathematical framework that quantifies privacy risk to individuals as a consequence of data collection and subsequent data release. — NIST SP 800-226 U.S. federal
Crosswalk → statistical disclosure limitation (formal) (instance_of): DP is a formal, provable form of SDL with a quantified privacy-accuracy trade-off. Disanalogy: traditional SDL (cell suppression, swapping) gives heuristic protection with no formal bound; DP gives a provable bound at an explicit, debated accuracy cost.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The Census Bureau's Disclosure Avoidance System applied formal differential privacy to 2020 Census products — the first nationwide federal use. NIST publishes two official definitions (SP 800-188 and SP 800-226); both are kept here. Politically contested inside Commerce (see DAO-216-26).
Cited in: eo_14110, acdeb_final_report_2022, m_25_05

de-identification

AI Core
also: de-identify
Stripping or masking the parts of a record that point to a specific person, so the data can be used without exposing identities. Note: de-identified is not the same as re-identification-proof — combinations of remaining fields can still single someone out.
Definition
General term for any process of removing the association between a set of identifying data and the data subject.
— NIST SP 800-188 (De-Identifying Government Datasets) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The umbrella practice under which suppression, swapping, noise injection, and differential privacy all sit. CIPSEA's 'identifiable form' standard (44 U.S.C. § 3561) is the legal target de-identification must defeat.
Cited in: cipsea_2018, m_19_23, pra_1995_uslm

re-identification

AI Core
also: re-identify
The reverse of de-identification: using leftover fields, outside data, or clever attacks to figure out who a 'de-identified' record actually is. FSS law defines the line in terms of whether identity can be 'reasonably inferred' by direct OR indirect means.
Definition
''identifiable form'' means any representation of information that permits the identity of the respondent to whom the information applies to be reasonably inferred by either direct or indirect means.
— CIPSEA 2018 §3561(7) — 'identifiable form' (the re-identification standard) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The legal harm CIPSEA confidentiality protections exist to prevent; the 'reasonably inferred by direct or indirect means' test is what disclosure-avoidance methods are measured against.
Cited in: sap_policy_m_23_04, wp_22, acdeb_final_report_2022

AI Governance & Risk

11 terms

high-impact AI

AI Governance & Risk ⚠ False friend
also: high-impact AI use case
The federal government's label for the AI uses that can actually hurt people or violate rights, which therefore get the strictest oversight. Under the current memo (M-25-21) this single 'high-impact' category replaced the older split of 'safety-impacting' vs 'rights-impacting' (M-24-10).
Definition
AI with an output that serves as a principal basis for decisions or actions that have a legal, material, binding, or significant effect on rights or safety.
— OMB M-25-21 (Apr. 3, 2025) U.S. federal
⚠ False friend — different in AI vs. the FSS
FALSE FRIEND: NIST's cybersecurity 'high-impact system' (FIPS 199) is about confidentiality/integrity/availability impact, NOT the OMB AI rights/safety sense. Don't conflate them.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Determines which agency AI tools trigger minimum risk-management practices (pre-deployment testing, impact assessment, ongoing monitoring). Most core statistical-production AI is not high-impact, but tools informing individual determinations can be.
Cited in: m_25_22, m_25_21, eo_14110

AI use case inventory

AI Governance & Risk
also: use case inventory
A yearly, public list every federal agency must publish of where it uses AI, flagging the high-impact uses and how it manages their risks. It is the main transparency mechanism for federal AI.
Definition
An annual, public inventory of an agency's AI use cases, identifying high-impact use cases and describing associated risks and mitigations, required of federal agencies.
— OMB M-25-21 (Apr. 3, 2025) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Statistical agencies must inventory production AI tools — imputation models, automated coding, disclosure-avoidance systems — beginning with the 2024 cycle.
Cited in: americas_ai_action_plan_2025, m_24_10, m_24_18

Chief AI Officer

AI Governance & Risk
also: CAIO
The senior official each agency must name to own its AI strategy, governance, and risk. In statistical agencies this role overlaps with the Chief Data Officer and Statistical Official.
Definition
A senior agency official designated under OMB AI guidance to coordinate and oversee the agency's use of AI, including its risk management and the AI use case inventory.
— OMB M-25-21 (Apr. 3, 2025); originally OMB M-24-10 U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The CAIO's remit intersects the CDO and Statistical Official; in an FSS agency, AI-assisted collection and dissemination create shared responsibility across these roles.
Cited in: m_24_10, m_25_21

AI impact assessment

AI Governance & Risk
also: AI impact analysis
A required write-up, before you turn an AI tool on (and repeated periodically), of what it's for, who it could harm, and how you'll manage that. The AI equivalent of a methods-and-limitations review.
Definition
A structured, pre-deployment and ongoing evaluation of an AI use case's intended purpose, expected benefit, and potential risks, required as a minimum practice for high-impact (formerly safety-/rights-impacting) AI.
— OMB M-25-21 (Apr. 3, 2025); cf. OMB M-24-10 §5 U.S. federal
Crosswalk → SPD No. 2 quality review / methodology review (analog_of): Both are structured pre-release reviews of accuracy, fitness, and risk. Disanalogy: an AI impact assessment centers rights/safety harms and bias, which a classical quality review does not foreground.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Principal statistical agencies must complete these for qualifying tools.
Cited in: m_25_21, m_25_22, nist_ai_rmf_100_1

algorithmic bias

AI Governance & Risk
also: AI bias
When an AI system is systematically wrong in a way that disadvantages a particular group — often because the training data or design baked in an existing skew. It is not random error; it is patterned and tends to track demographic lines.
Definition
Systematic and repeatable errors in an AI system that create unfair outcomes, such as privileging one group over another; arising from data, modeling choices, or human/societal factors (NIST distinguishes systemic, statistical/computational, and human-cognitive bias).
— NIST SP 1270 (Towards a Standard for Identifying and Managing Bias in AI) U.S. federal
Crosswalk → nonresponse bias / differential measurement error (analog_of): Both are systematic (not random) distortions that hit specific subgroups. Disanalogy: nonresponse bias has an established estimation/adjustment theory; algorithmic bias often lacks a measurable true value to adjust toward.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Central risk when AI is used for demographic estimation, record linkage, or automated classification; can deepen undercoverage of underrepresented groups.
Cited in: nist_ai_rmf_100_1, nist_ai_rmf_playbook

AI red-teaming

AI Governance & Risk
also: red-teaming, red team
Deliberately attacking your own AI system — feeding it adversarial or edge-case inputs — to find where it fails, produces biased or harmful output, or can be manipulated, before real-world use. AI red-teaming adapts the cybersecurity practice to model behavior, not just security.
Definition
A group of people authorized and organized to emulate a potential adversary’s attack or exploitation capabilities against an enterprise’s security posture. The Red Team’s objective is to improve enterprise cybersecurity by demonstrating the impacts of successful attacks and by demonstrating what works for the defenders (i.e., the Blue Team) in an operational environment. Also known as Cyber Red Team.
— CNSSI 4009-2015 (cybersecurity sense) U.S. federal
Crosswalk → cognitive testing / respondent debriefing / reinterview (analog_of): Both adversarially probe an instrument for failure modes before production. Disanalogy: cognitive testing studies human comprehension of questions; AI red-teaming studies model behavior under adversarial inputs.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Can stress-test FSS AI tools for demographic bias in classification, failure on edge-case data, or manipulation of public-facing NLP systems.
Cited in: eo_14110, m_24_10, m_24_18

explainability

AI Governance & Risk
also: interpretability, explainable AI
Whether you can say, in terms a human can follow, WHY the AI produced a given output. A model can be accurate but unexplainable (a 'black box'), which is a problem when you must defend a methodology publicly.
Definition
A property of AI systems concerning the degree to which the reasons for an output can be represented in terms meaningful to a given audience; one of the trustworthy-AI characteristics (explainable and interpretable).
— NIST AI 100-1 (AI Risk Management Framework 1.0) U.S. federal
Crosswalk → methodology documentation / transparency (SPD No. 2) (analog_of): Both make a result auditable and reproducible to outsiders. Disanalogy: methodology docs describe a designed procedure; explainability must reconstruct the behavior of a learned model that has no hand-written procedure.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
FSS uses of AI for record linkage, imputation, or small-area estimation must be explainable to data users and oversight bodies, consistent with SPD No. 2 transparency expectations.
Cited in: americas_ai_action_plan_2025, m_25_22, nist_ai_rmf_100_1

trustworthy AI

AI Governance & Risk ⚠ False friend
also: responsible AI
NIST's checklist of the seven properties an AI system needs to be worthy of trust. It is the evaluative baseline federal agencies use to judge whether an AI tool is acceptable.
Definition
AI that is valid and reliable, safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable and interpretable, privacy-enhanced, and fair with harmful bias managed.
— NIST AI 100-1 (AI Risk Management Framework 1.0) U.S. federal
⚠ False friend — different in AI vs. the FSS
FALSE FRIEND: NIST's cybersecurity 'trustworthiness' is about security/reliability of an information system; 'trustworthy AI' is the seven-characteristic AI-RMF construct. Different frameworks.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The seven NIST characteristics form the assessment baseline for AI tools deployed by principal statistical agencies.
Cited in: eo_14110, m_24_10, m_25_21

confabulation

AI Governance & Risk ⚠ False friend
also: fabrication
When a generative model states something false as if it were true — invented citations, wrong numbers, made-up facts — fluently and without warning. NIST prefers 'confabulation' over 'hallucination' because the model isn't perceiving anything; it is filling a gap with a plausible-sounding guess.
Definition
The production of confidently stated but erroneous or false content (also called 'hallucination' or 'fabrication') by a generative-AI system; identified by NIST as a pervasive generative-AI risk.
— NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) U.S. federal
⚠ False friend — different in AI vs. the FSS
Both are content errors — a reported value departs from the truth. Disanalogy: measurement error is bounded around a true value and modeled as a distribution; confabulation invents referents that do not exist, so it has no true value to be bounded around.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Critical risk when LLMs draft statistical reports, generate metadata, or summarize findings; mandates human verification before any figure or claim is relied on. FALSE FRIEND: an FSS reader may hear 'confabulation/hallucination' as harmless creativity; it is a specific risk of confident fabrication, and it is NOT measurement error (it can invent referents).
Cited in: m_24_18, webb_nist_fcsm_crosswalk, m_19_23

information integrity

AI Governance & Risk
The danger that AI-made content pollutes the information environment with convincing but false or manipulative material. For a statistical agency, the worry is AI-drafted public text that misstates the data.
Definition
The risk that AI-generated content undermines accurate, truthful, and reliable information — including disinformation, propaganda, or manipulative content; generative-AI risk category in NIST AI 600-1.
— NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) U.S. federal
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Applies when agencies use generative AI for press releases, data-product narratives, or public-facing summaries — official statistics carry an authority that misinformation can borrow.
Cited in: nist_genai_profile_600_1

automated decision system

AI Governance & Risk
also: ADS, automated decision-making
A computer process that makes a consequential decision about a person — or substantially drives a human's decision — with little or no meaningful human judgment (e.g. auto-approving or flagging a case). The concern is consequential decisions handed to software without meaningful human review.
Definition
a machine-based system that can, for a given set of human-defined objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments.
— 15 U.S.C. § 9401(3) (National AI Initiative Act of 2020) U.S. federal
Also defined: OMB M-24-10 uses 'automated decision-making systems' as an operative term — e.g. in its definition of automation bias: 'the propensity for humans to inordinately favor suggestions from automated decision-making systems and to ignore or fail to seek out contradictory information.' — OMB M-24-10 (federal AI-governance usage) U.S. federal
Also defined: any technology that either assists or replaces the judgement of human decision-makers. — Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat, Directive on Automated Decision-Making (2021) intl. government
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
No single U.S.-federal statutory definition of 'automated decision system' exists. The U.S.-federal anchors are the AI statutory definition (15 U.S.C. § 9401(3) — cited directly, NOT via the revoked EO 14110 that quotes it) and OMB AI-governance usage (M-24-10); the Canadian Treasury Board directive is the cleanest concise operational definition. Relevant in the FSS where data products feed individual-level determinations; pair with human-in-the-loop requirements.
Cited in: m_24_10

FSS Bridge

9 terms

disclosure avoidance

FSS Bridge ⚖ Contested
also: disclosure limitation
The whole set of methods agencies apply before releasing statistics so that no individual respondent can be identified — suppression, rounding, swapping, noise injection, differential privacy. Required by the confidentiality statutes.
Definition
The set of methods to reduce the risk of disclosing information on individuals, businesses or other organizations. Such methods are only related to the dissemination step and are usually based on restricting the amount of or modifying the data released.
— NIST SP 800-188 (statistical disclosure control) U.S. federal · contested
Crosswalk → differential privacy / cell suppression / swapping (umbrella_of): Disclosure avoidance is the goal; DP, suppression, swapping, and noise injection are alternative methods that achieve it with different utility/privacy trade-offs.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Required by Title 13, CIPSEA, and agency statutes. The Census Bureau's Disclosure Avoidance System (DAS) implemented differential privacy at scale for the 2020 Census. The choice of method is politically contested inside Commerce (see DAO-216-26).
Cited in: acdeb_final_report_2022, cipsea_2002_implementation_guidance, fcsm_20_04

statistical disclosure limitation

FSS Bridge
also: SDL, statistical disclosure control
The technical name for the family of methods that reduce the risk that a published statistic reveals information about a specific individual or business. In the FSS this is the methodological core of disclosure avoidance.
Definition
The set of methods to reduce the risk of disclosing information on individuals, businesses or other organizations. Such methods are only related to the dissemination step and are usually based on restricting the amount of or modifying the data released.
— NIST SP 800-188 U.S. federal
Crosswalk → disclosure avoidance (synonym_near): Used nearly interchangeably; 'disclosure avoidance' is the Census Bureau's operational term, 'statistical disclosure limitation/control' the methodological literature's term.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
The canonical FSS reference is FCSM Statistical Policy Working Paper 22, 'Report on Statistical Disclosure Limitation Methodology' (in this corpus as wp_22). Differential privacy is a formal member of this family.
Cited in: acdeb_final_report_2022, m_25_05, wp_22

measurement error

FSS Bridge
The gap between the answer recorded and the true value, caused by the respondent, interviewer, question wording, mode, or records. One of the components of nonsampling error.
Definition
Measurement error is the difference between the true value of the measurement and the value obtained during the measurement process.
— U.S. Census Bureau Statistical Quality Standards — Glossary U.S. federal
Crosswalk → confabulation (generative AI) (analog_of): Both are content errors relative to a true value. Disanalogy: measurement error is bounded by a true value; confabulation can invent non-existent referents.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
A core FSS quality concept (U.S. Census Bureau Statistical Quality Standards; the D3 standard in this corpus is about producing measures of it). Its AI relevance: NLP tools parsing open-ended responses can introduce systematic measurement error that varies by respondent group, which is the bridge to AI confabulation/bias.
Cited in: fcsm_20_04, wp_31, wp_47

nonsampling error

FSS Bridge
Every source of error in a statistic that is NOT from random sampling — coverage gaps, nonresponse, measurement error, processing mistakes. Often the dominant error in large surveys, and harder to quantify than sampling error.
Definition
Nonsampling errors are survey errors caused by factors other than sampling (e.g., nonsampling errors include errors in coverage, response errors, nonresponse errors, faulty questionnaires, interviewer recording errors, and processing errors).
— U.S. Census Bureau Statistical Quality Standards — Glossary U.S. federal
Crosswalk → model error / bias + drift (AI) (analog_of): Nonsampling error is the FSS's mature taxonomy of non-random error; AI bias, drift, and confabulation map onto its branches (coverage, temporal, measurement).
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus

AI-ready data

FSS Bridge ★ Flagship
also: AI-readiness, AI-ready
Data is “AI-ready” when a machine — typically an LLM — can not just open it but actually understand it: the numbers arrive with enough context, documentation, provenance, and quality information that the model interprets them correctly and answers questions without mangling the statistics. Machine-readable (a clean CSV) is necessary but not sufficient; machine-understandable is the bar. Note this assumes a system can already get to the data — whether it can find and retrieve it in the first place is a separate question (see the addendum).
Definition
Data is AI-ready when it is machine-understandable — not merely machine-readable: its context, provenance, methodology, and quality signals are preserved and programmatically queryable, sufficient for an AI system (particularly a large language model) to analyze and answer questions about the asset without loss of statistical integrity. Per the federal project definition, AI-readiness is “the extent to which a data asset is prepared for effective analysis and querying by AI systems, particularly LLMs,” spanning data quality, access, formatting, and metadata. Scope boundary: this definition assumes access to the data asset is already established — it addresses the readiness of data that a consuming system can already reach. (The discoverability/retrievability axis is treated separately in the forward-interpretation addendum, deliberately outside the grounded definition.)
— America’s DataHub RFS — Topic MLMU-25, “Measuring Large Language Model Understanding of Federal Statistical Data” (footnote 2); framed as an extension of FCSM 25-03 (AI-Ready Federal Statistical Data) and FCSM 20-04 (A Framework for Data Quality). U.S. federal
Scope boundary: Definition (Part A) covers the readiness of data a consuming system can already REACH; it assumes access is established. The discoverability/retrievability (access) axis is addressed only in the forward-interpretation addendum (Part B), explicitly ahead of policy.
Also defined: For the purposes of this project, “AI-readiness” refers to the extent to which a data asset is prepared for effective analysis and querying by AI systems, particularly LLMs. This includes data quality, access, formatting, and metadata. AI-ready data enable LLMs to generate meaningful insights from both structured (e.g., tabular) and unstructured (e.g., reports) formats by facilitating accurate interpretation and contextual understanding. — America’s DataHub RFS, Topic MLMU-25, footnote 2 (verbatim, verified against source 2026-06-23) U.S. federal
Crosswalk → Lawrence Data Readiness Levels (2017), Bands C/B/A — academic conceptual ancestor (arXiv:1705.02245) (descends_from): Lawrence’s Data Readiness Levels are the conceptual ancestor: Band C = accessibility (can the data be accessed), Band B = faithfulness/validity (is it what it claims to be), Band A = data-in-context (ready for a specific, defined task). AI-readiness inherits the use-case-conditional logic — Lawrence shows readiness is relative to the intended use (this use-case-conditionality point is Lawrence’s, 2017; it is NOT attributed to Gartner). Disanalogy: DRL is a general-purpose data-maturity scale; this definition is scoped to federal statistical data and adds the “without loss of statistical integrity” constraint a general maturity scale does not carry. Cited as an external ancestor only — academic, not federal policy, so not ingested into the corpus.
✓ Grounded in the FSS corpus
Grounded in the corpus: the federal definition (America’s DataHub / NSDS RFS) plus the FCSM 25-03 AI-ready extension and the FCSM 20-04 data-quality framework. The corpus establishes that creating AI-ready data “builds on established open-data principles” and machine-readability (FCSM 25-03 §s34), that federal statistics used in AI interactions must meet the Framework’s data-quality dimensions — accessibility, timeliness, accuracy (§s30) — and that metadata enrichment exposed through AI-friendly APIs improves how LLMs discover and interpret data (§s31). FCSM 20-04 §s184 anchors the data-quality dimensions that must be balanced and preserved.
Cited in: bea_rfs_mlmu25, fcsm_25_03, fcsm_25_03, fcsm_25_03, fcsm_20_04
⏩ Forward interpretation — ahead of documented policy (NOT an established standard) · dated 2026-06Practitioner framing (NOT graph-grounded; ahead of the documented standard): the federal and Commerce/FCSM definitions above assume access to the data asset is already established. In practice, access and machine-retrievability must be deliberately configured — via emerging mechanisms such as llms.txt, the Model Context Protocol (MCP), and WebMCP, the last standardized only around 2026, i.e. AFTER the FCSM/Commerce literature was authored. Written policy lags the technology in this fast-moving field. A complete AI-data-readiness picture therefore treats discoverability and retrievability as a PRIOR axis the current standards do not yet cover: data a model cannot find or reach is not AI-ready in any operational sense, however machine-understandable it is once reached. This is the machine-as-first-class-user position, offered as a forward-looking, bounded thought-leadership seed — not a mandate, and deliberately separated from the grounded definition so the definition’s authority is not diluted by interpretation.
Builds on (does not supersede): FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable); FCSM 20-04 (A Framework for Data Quality); FCSM 25-03 (AI-Ready Federal Statistical Data). FAIR — AI-readiness builds on / extends FAIR; it does not supersede it.
