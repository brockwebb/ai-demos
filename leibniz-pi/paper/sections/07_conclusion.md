# 7. Conclusion

The Leibniz series is discoverable by genetic programming from arithmetic primitives and an information-theoretic fitness function. The fitness need not mention π, alternating signs, or odd denominators: these emerge as the simplest structure satisfying sustained constant-rate information gain. Discovery is reliable under constrained search spaces and fails sharply as the search space expands. The scaling grid (t = 4 through t = 20, populations 1,000 through 10,000) places the phase transition boundary between t=8 and t=10 for all tested populations. At t=4, success is reliable. At t=10 and above, no population size up to 10,000 achieves consistent discovery. The anomalous partial recovery at t=15 with pop=10,000 (2/5 seeds) does not contradict this pattern. It suggests that sufficiently large populations can occasionally maintain the structural diversity needed to escape wrong-limit attractor basins, but cannot do so reliably.

The failure is not in the fitness landscape. Leibniz sits at or near the global optimum of the information-theoretic fitness in all tested configurations. The fitness correctly ranks Leibniz above every wrong-limit attractor when both are present. The question is harder than the answer: whether the search can cover enough of the space for correct building blocks to appear before wrong-limit attractors occupy and hold the population.

This framing, that discovery is governed by the ratio of coverage to search space rather than by fitness quality, has consequences for symbolic regression on infinite-horizon problems generally. Richer terminal sets, larger search spaces, and more expressive operators all increase the density of exploitable wrong-limit attractors faster than any population increase can compensate. Fitness engineering alone cannot solve the coverage problem. Addressing it would require constrained search spaces, structured initialization that guarantees coverage of known building blocks, or evaluation horizons long enough to distinguish near-correct from exactly-correct convergence. The evaluation horizon trap analysis suggests the last of these is an asymptotically receding target.
