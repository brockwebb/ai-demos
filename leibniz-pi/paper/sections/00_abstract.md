# Abstract

We investigate whether genetic programming can discover the Leibniz series for π/4 from arithmetic primitives alone, without prior knowledge of the target formula. Using a log-precision fitness function that rewards constant precision gain per decade of summation depth, a minimal GP system discovers Leibniz-equivalent expressions in {{result:logprec_minimal_5_5:value}}/5 seeds with the terminal set {k, 1, -1, 2}. Discovery fails sharply as the terminal set expands: at 15 terminals and population 1,000, no seeds succeed ({{result:logprec_stress_l1_0_5:value}}/5) despite the fitness correctly ranking Leibniz as optimal when present.

We characterize this phase transition through a scaling grid of terminal counts and population sizes. The primary failure mechanism is wrong-limit attractors: series that converge to values near π/4 and can score higher than Leibniz within any finite evaluation horizon. These attractors are structurally simpler than Leibniz and cannot be eliminated by fitness engineering. Six fitness modifications all failed at 15 terminals, confirming that the bottleneck is combinatorial coverage of correct building blocks, not fitness landscape quality.

The log-precision fitness measures -log₂(|error|), rewarding processes whose precision grows at a constant rate across scales. Leibniz gains {{result:info_rate_3_32:value}} bits per decade, the rate predicted by its 1/T error decay. By analogy to reaction kinetics, the fitness selects for second-order convergence structure, a more discriminating criterion than first-order error reduction. The difficulty of formula discovery lies not in the answer but in the search: whether the population covers enough of the space for correct building blocks to appear before wrong-limit attractors dominate.
