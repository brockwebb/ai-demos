# 1. Introduction

The Leibniz series

$$\frac{\pi}{4} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{2k+1} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \cdots$$

converges to π/4 but does so slowly: roughly 5 billion terms are required for 10 digits of precision. Far more efficient algorithms exist for computing π. The series is famous not for its efficiency but for its structural elegance, a process whose error decreases algebraically as 1/T, gaining roughly {{result:info_rate_3_32:value}} bits of precision per decade of terms.

This paper asks a constructive question: given only arithmetic primitives and a fitness criterion that rewards sustained convergence, can an evolutionary search discover Leibniz from scratch? After correcting an initial methodological confound in which the target expression was inadvertently seeded in the population (Section 4.1), all experiments use pure random initialization. The answer is yes when the terminal set is small enough that correct building blocks dominate the search space, and no when additional terminals drown the signal in combinatorial alternatives. The failure conditions are more instructive than the success.

Symbolic regression typically searches for equations that fit observed data points. The Leibniz problem differs: there are no data points. The fitness must evaluate a *process*, a generating rule applied at every integer k. The question is whether that process exhibits the convergence properties characteristic of a series summing to a mathematical constant. This shifts the optimization objective from pointwise accuracy to process-level behavior: not "how close is the output?" but "is the output still improving, and will it always be?"

We develop two fitness functions for this objective. The convergence-aware fitness rewards expressions whose partial sums decrease in error between evaluation checkpoints. The log-precision fitness rewards expressions that gain bits of precision about π/4 at a constant rate per decade of summation depth, motivated by an analogy to reaction kinetics developed in Section 6.1. Both discover Leibniz under favorable conditions. The log-precision fitness is more reliable, succeeding at half the population size.

The central finding is not that Leibniz can be discovered, but that discovery exhibits a sharp phase transition as the search space grows. With 4 terminals, discovery is reliable. With 15, it fails completely. The fitness function does not break and correctly identifies Leibniz as optimal when Leibniz is present. The failure is that the search space becomes populated with wrong-limit attractors: expressions whose partial sums converge to finite values near π/4 within the evaluation horizon, but whose true limit is not π/4. Wrong-limit attractors are structurally simpler than Leibniz. No improvement to the fitness function can eliminate them, because no finite evaluation can distinguish "converges to π/4" from "converges to a nearby value." The failure mode parallels confabulation in language models: outputs that appear correct within a finite evaluation horizon but do not correspond to the true target (Section 6.3).

This evaluation horizon trap has implications beyond the Leibniz problem. Any fitness-guided search over infinite-horizon processes faces the same constraint. The difficulty of discovery is not in the fitness landscape, which places Leibniz at or near its global optimum. The difficulty is in coverage: the fraction of structurally distinct building blocks present in the population at any generation. When coverage is insufficient, wrong-limit attractors dominate before the correct sub-expressions can assemble.
